History


Before Bhutan was consolidated into a kingdom, it consisted of many small principalities.
The regions had names like:

  • Lho Jong – "The Valley of the South"
  • Lho Mon Kha Shi – "The Southern Mon Land with the Four Approaches"
  • Lho Jong Men Jong – "The Southern Valley of Medicinal Herbs"
  • Lho Mon Tsenden Jong – "The Southern Mon Valley Where Sandalwood Grows"

First British travelers brought this name, which is believed to derive from the Sanskrit word "Bhot-anta" (Bhot = Tibet, anta = the end), meaning "the end of Tibet." Bhutanese themselves have always referred to their country as "Druk-Yul" – Land of the Thunder Dragon.

Songtsen Gampo or Songzan Ganbu, was the 33rd Tibetan king and founder of the Tibetan Empire.


Early Civilization

Bhutan's history traces back to around 2000 BC, supported by archaeological evidence like stone tools and structures. The state of Lhomon, possibly existing between 500 BC and AD 600, has been theorized. Buddhism was introduced in the 7th century, with King Songtsen Gampo playing a crucial role. Early records were lost in a fire in 1827, but by the 10th century, Bhutan's religious history significantly influenced its politics. The country faced internal conflicts, and the Drukpa Lineage rose to prominence by the 16th century.

European Contact

Portuguese Jesuits visited Bhutan in 1627, marking the first European contact. Internal conflicts persisted, leading to wars with neighboring regions. Bhutan faced British intervention in the 18th century, culminating in the Duar War (1864–65) and the signing of the Treaty of Sinchula. In the late 19th century, civil war ensued, and Ugyen Wangchuck emerged victorious, becoming the hereditary king in 1907. Bhutan signed the Treaty of Punakha in 1910, aligning itself with British India. In 1953, King Jigme Dorji Wangchuck initiated democratic reforms, and in 1971, Bhutan joined the United Nations. King Jigme Singye Wangchuck ascended to the throne in 1972.

Bhutan's Immigration Concerns and Refugee Crisis

The 1980s saw Bhutan implementing policies to safeguard its cultural identity, including a census in 1988 to address concerns of illegal immigration. This led to protests, and the government responded with increased resistance. The census was used to identify and evict a significant number of Lhotshampas, resulting in their becoming refugees in southern Nepal. Western countries resettled many of these refugees since 2008.
